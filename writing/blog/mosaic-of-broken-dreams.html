Mosaic of Broken Dreams

There’s a specific kind of ache that comes with failure—a chip in your self-esteem, because you were so sure things would work out. That kind of hypocrisy can make you want to disappear. You can’t go back and change the past into what you had hoped. And when you look around, there's nothing tangible to show for the time — no accomplishments to display, at least not by the world’s usual standards.

The hardest part about failure, for me, is the expectations of others, or at least the beliefs I had about other people’s expectations of me. It’s the casual conversations that bring up the past and the awkward discussions about the future. Speaking the truth when it’s a disappointment to others is another goal I’m chipping away at.

I’ve learned to see past my own failures and missed opportunities to find the lessons life had to teach me. It’s not always easy for others to see your point of view. I’m learning to deal with that.

It can be difficult to dig deep through the feelings of regret, but doing so, with an honest look at all the pieces, can give you hope for the future. The dream that fell through may just need to be pursued differently. Or maybe you look closer to find where the idea came from, and if it was even authentically yours to begin with. Some aspects may still be there, and maybe life can provide a new path forward, if you are willing to search for it.

I was once offered an opportunity that felt like a dream. I started walking a path that looked good on the surface, but was tangled by the influence of others. I felt that something was off, but the excitement of it all swept away my concerns like a whirlwind. At the time I didn’t understand what success would entail or how to make the most of my situation. I only had an idealized fantasy of what my life could look like, while letting others lead the way.

It can be a blessing and a curse to see potential so clearly—especially when it’s easier to recognize in others than in yourself. I understood the essence of what I wanted, but on a practical level, my path wasn’t aligned.

I’m adaptable, which has served me well in many roles, making it easy to mold my ideas into another person’s objective. I was used to relying on other people’s vision while serving their mission. It works well in the structured environments I was familiar with, but my first attempt at entrepreneurship had me clinging to what I knew. I wasn’t comfortable trusting my own instincts, which left me feeling too afraid to forge my own path.

Recognizing potential in collaboration was a skill I needed to develop. It can be hard to understand the motives of another because not everyone has the same intentions as you. In fact, people often carry intentions that aren’t always obvious at first. There may be points of potential somewhere along the line, but it's necessary to understand what those are before committing to long-term goals.

Knowing yourself and having a clear goal is essential. I’ve learned to explore my intentions and objectives more honestly, finding my own discrepancies. I’m not separate from the people I meet — an ideology I’ve had to accept, even when I don’t want to.

I didn’t fail because success was out of reach—I failed because I didn’t fully understand what success meant to me. I had a vision of who I thought I would be, and an imagination that clouded my judgment. There are parts of myself that could never align to that fictitious character I created in my mind. The beauty was discovering how these parts of myself I wanted to ignore could be embraced. Learning how to work with them — rather than against them — was the only way forward.

We can’t ignore who we really are, or try to fit into someone else’s story if we have our own to tell. Trying to shape another person’s path into our vision only leads to something distorted.

It doesn’t mean that the encounters leading us off one path are failures, either. Often life presents us with opportunities to reflect what we want, and things we may never want to experience again.

And as for the broken dreams that still hold a place in your heart — they deserve to be examined, not discarded. There are reasons for everything that makes its way into your life… whether that’s a vision, something tangible and sentimental, people, or even pets.

Owning your story is healing. Not the polished version, but the real one. The one where you didn’t have it all together. The one that didn’t go according to plan.

Finding hope means picking up the pieces that still matter and putting them together — this time forming something new. Something with cracks and color, full of depth.

A one-of-a-kind mosaic, beautifully built from something once broken.
