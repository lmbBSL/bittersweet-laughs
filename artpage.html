An Art Journey

I didn't follow a traditional path, but I wasn't exactly self-taught either. I remember being a child and pausing The Lion King over and over again, rewinding the tape just right so I could pause it on the same frame I had been sketching from before.

The Early Years

As a child, when I was asked what I wanted to be when I grew up, I said I wanted to be an actress, a model, or an artist. Naturally, we lose sight of childhood dreams as we become adults. But they never completely disappear—even if the vision of them changes.

Eventually, I enrolled in a college theatre program. At first, I wanted to be on the stage, but I found myself in the paint shop much more frequently. That's where I learned the fun things about paint—different techniques, texture, color theory, and how much possibility lives in a few simple materials.

I love mixing colors and rarely use a color straight from the tube. I remember sitting in the shop for hours. Even painting the boring stuff, like flat gray on various set pieces, was soothing.

Finding My Way Back

Years later, after spending time pursuing acting, I found myself drawn inward, seeking other ways to stay creative.

That's when I began painting pictures using photographs I had taken on a trip and some old craft paints left over from my holiday ceramic-painting hobby. I loved dragging the colors together and watching new shades emerge.

One of my first paintings came from a photo I took at a little bed and breakfast on Solomons Island, Maryland. A few months later, I turned that image into a painting, then I created a few more.

It wasn’t something I did all the time. Much like I am now, I go through phases. Sometimes I like to work quietly, away from the world, in my studio. Other times, I like to be out in the world exploring and capturing my experiences with a camera. I often paint from the photos I’ve taken.

Slowly, I began taking over the guest room in our house. Eventually, I created an in-home studio, but I've moved several times since then, changing what that looks like.

There were times when everything I had to create with fit inside a duffel bag I carried with me, along with a selection of watercolors and art prints to sell.

The Sint Maarten Years

In August of 2019, I moved to Sint Maarten to teach art at a foundation.

Things didn't go quite as planned.

During the lockdown, like so many people, I found myself reconsidering what came next. I decided to stay on the island and eventually started my own business, The Party Palette.

We bring art to the party was the idea—but it was only me.

I imagined classes, events, and creative experiences, but I soon discovered that people were often interested in my own artwork. I began making prints and creating new pieces inspired by Sint Maarten—the beaches, hills, buildings, light, and everyday scenes around me.

When Art Became a Livelihood

My art journey has evolved from a creative outlet during a time of quiet contemplation, into a pursued profession that supported me, and back into a hobby — something I turn to not only when I feel the urge to create, but as a tried and true process for self-regulation.

I discovered that when my art became a means of livelihood, a subtle pressure settled over my work. Creating art became a different process once I began to focus on popular products. I began creating magnets and other small souveniers, giving customers a little piece of original art to take home.

Many of my customers were tourists, but I was happy to recieve support from several local people as well. I started branching out, completing a few murals for some small businesses, and I even took on a commission to paint hats for a local boutique.

I began to think of painting differently. It was a technical skill that I could use to support myself and it didn't matter if I wasn't making my art. I began to appreciate my own work even more, remembering when I could spend days and even weeks on one painting with no external pressure. I missed the creative freedom.

It was then I realized that it doesn't matter what work you do in life, what matters is how much you value yourself and the work you do. I was always happy when customers were pleased with my work, but its even more special when someone connects with the piece I was inspired to create.

In the Studio Now

Currently, I have a shared space to live and work. It’s important for me to have my materials easily accessible, with a place to leave my creative mess while I am tending to life.

I will soon have a shop available for purchasing art — both original pieces and printed products. I’ve learned that my creative process is meant to be savored. I am open to commissions when the work resonates with me.

Currently, I have been working on small mosaics, and I am looking forward to the warmer months when I can find a sunny spot to lay out my watercolors and take in the scenery around me.
